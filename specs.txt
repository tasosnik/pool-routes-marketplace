You are acting as a senior product engineer, product architect, and technical founder. Your job is not just to plan, but to actually build.

Objective:
Design and implement a high-quality local-first prototype of “PoolRoute OS,” a mobile-friendly software platform for buying, integrating, operating, and analyzing pool service routes. The highest-priority user outcomes are:

1. A user can upload or enter their existing routes/accounts.
2. A user can evaluate a route for sale and visualize how it fits into their existing portfolio.

Everything else is secondary to those two outcomes.

Use plan mode for architecture, roadmap, tradeoff decisions, and major refactors. After planning, switch into implementation and keep the product runnable locally at all times.

Working style and execution requirements:
- Do not stop at analysis or planning.
- Continue iterating until you have built a working product, not just documents or mockups.
- After each meaningful implementation step, reflect on what you built, identify the biggest weaknesses or missing pieces, and continue iterating.
- Keep going until the result is coherent, usable, and locally testable.
- Prefer shipping a narrower but working product over a broader but incomplete one.
- Make reasonable assumptions when needed and document them.
- Do not wait for approval between steps unless blocked by a hard external dependency.

Mandatory workflow:
1. First, understand the product and produce:
   - a concise product brief,
   - a phased roadmap (MVP, V1, V2+),
   - a technical architecture,
   - a prioritized backlog broken into implementation tickets similar to Jira stories.

5. Once something meaningful works locally:
   - commit changes clearly,
   - push to Git,
   - open/update a PR if appropriate,
   - then continue iterating with additional improvements.
6. Repeat this cycle until the product is solid.

Product requirements:

Product name:
PoolRoute OS

Core concept:
A trusted, mobile-friendly operating system and marketplace for pool service routes, where operators can upload current routes, evaluate routes for acquisition, visualize overlap and operational fit, and eventually manage post-acquisition service operations.

Primary priority:
The user should be able to:
1. upload/import their existing routes,
2. visualize those routes on a map,
3. import or evaluate a route for sale,
4. see what their portfolio would look like if they acquired that route.

Secondary capabilities:
A. Route Marketplace
- Sellers can list routes for sale.
- Routes may include account count, geography, revenue, service notes, payment history, and churn history.
- Buyers can evaluate listings with more trust and structure than brokers/Facebook groups.
- Escrow and tranche logic should be considered in architecture, even if mocked in MVP.

Example:
A seller posts 30 accounts in Pasadena generating $45K/year. A buyer sees 12 months of payment history and service logs, pays through escrow, and retention is tracked before funds are fully released.

B. Integration of New Routes into Existing Portfolio
- Users can upload existing routes/accounts into their account.
- Inputs may include:
  - manual text entry,
  - CSV,
  - PDF parsing,
  - image/photo OCR,
  - possibly voice memo transcription if appropriate.
- Users can visualize existing routes on a map.
- Users can upload or load a candidate acquisition route and compare:
  - geography,
  - overlap,
  - drive-time implications,
  - portfolio composition after acquisition.

This is the most important feature area.

C. Service Operations Management
- Route scheduling and optimization.
- Mobile-friendly technician workflow.
- Checklists, chemical logs, photo capture.
- Customer communication after service.
- Pricing intelligence / underpriced account nudges.

D. Post-Sale Retention & Guarantees
- Track retention after acquisition.
- Detect churn signals such as cancellations, complaints, or payment failures.
- Support escrow adjustment/refund logic if churn exceeds thresholds.

E. Financial Analytics & Valuation
- Automated route valuation model.
- Dashboard for MRR/revenue, cost ratios, productivity.
- Exportable P&L/reporting.

F. Future extensibility
- The architecture should make it possible to extend later into adjacent route-based service industries, especially commercial landscaping.

Product examples to keep in mind:
1. Route marketplace:
A seller lists 30 accounts in Pasadena generating $45K/year. Buyer sees payment history and service logs. Buyer pays $150K via escrow. Retention is tracked and funds are released in tranches.

2. Route integration:
A buyer uploads their current accounts, then uploads a target acquisition route, and sees both on a map with overlap, density, and operational impact.

3. Service operations:
A tech gets an optimized daily list with pool checklists, chemical logs, photos, and completion notifications to homeowners.

4. Retention guarantee:
If acquired accounts churn in the first 60–90 days, payout adjustments are triggered based on pre-defined rules.
